I spent most of my PhD studying carbon deposits in film cooled liquid rocket engines. My arrival at Purdue conveniently coincided with the start of a test program. I was greeted with a completely empty test cell that had just been refloored and a set of program goals promised in my advisor's funded proposal. My first year was spent designing and building fluids, structural, and electrical systems to support combustor testing. In parallel, I created a 4 MPa heat-sink cooled kerosene and hydrogen peroxide combustor to fulfill the requirements set forth in our statement of work. I led buildup, testing, and data analysis while taking on a project manager role for the experimental side of the program. During that test program, I designed a 20 MPa water cooled kerosene and oxygen combustor to more closely approach the conditions expected in a staged combustion engine. I was able to complete checkout testing on this higher pressure combustor, but funding ran out before the full test matrix could be finished.

Kerosene and H2O2 Combustor

The kerosene and hydrogen peroxide combustor (unaffectionately named the "Task 1" combustor) was designed to investigate carbon deposits in a film cooled combustor. Most rocket engines use fuel film cooling to keep the metal walls cool from typically greater than 3500 K flame temperatures. Kerosene fuels form carbon deposits on the metal chamber walls, which further insulate the walls from hot chamber gases. Most kerosene-based rocket engines use film cooling and account for it in their design, but none account for carbon deposit insulation on the wall, nor try to promote deposition for further insulation. This combustor was designed with a removable annular sample in a heat-sink cooled copper chamber such that the carbonaceous deposits could be analyzed after testing.

Flow through the combustor started with 90wt% hydrogen peroxide injected through a 25 mm inner diameter silver-based catalyst bed to decompose the H2O2 to approximately 1030 K hot oxygen and steam. An orifice plate with 16 holes prevented backflow of carbon-containing combustion products that could be caused by small pressure instabilities in the combustion chamber. After a slight contraction in the chamber diameter, fuel was radially injected through four holes into the hot oxygen and steam flow to promote mixing at a stoichiometric mixture ratio of 7.25. A rearward-facing axisymmetric step further promoted mixing while acting as a flame holder, increasing the internal dimaeter from 20 to 30 mm. A thin yttria-stabilized zirconia thermal barrier coating protected the flame holder section of the combustor from the high-enthalpy combustion gases, which had temperatures up to 2800 K when operated near stoichiometric conditions. Downstream of the step, a fuel film injector introduced a low-velocity annular kerosene flow through a thin axisymmetric gap to cool the inner wall of an annular sample chamber liner. The sample length was long enough that phase transition and carbon deposition from the fuel film occurred on the sample. A graphite converging-divergizing nozzle with a 114 mm throat diameter choked the flow to achieve chamber pressures up to 4.8 MPa

The combustor was operated in two modes: monopropellant and bipropellant mode. In monopropellant mode only hydrogen peroxide propellant was injected through the catalyst bed, while high pressure nitrogen was purged through the fuel injectors to prevent backflow of hot oxygen. Chamber pressures for monopropellant mode were approximately half those of bipropellant mode. The main fuel and fuel film injector were switched to kerosene during bipropellant mode, which always followed a brief period of monopropellant mode to verify catalyst bed operation. The shutdown procedure consisted of purging nitrogen through the fuel orifices first, followed by hydrogen peroxide shutdown second, all occurring within a few tenths of a second. Total test times were typically capped at around 15 seconds, although later testing in a vacuum chamber completed test times up to 30 seconds.

The combustor was largely designed for removable sample access, with minimal chamber pressure and hardware temperature measurements. However, fifty thermocouples were added halfway through the test matrix to allow for inverse heat transfer measurements of heat flux, heat transfer coefficient, and carbon deposit properties. The thermocouples were adhered into blind holes within the chamber, with consistent thermal contact resistance achieved at the thermocouple tips. I published a journal paper detailing how this 1D inverse problem could be solved in real time through a method that took advantage of a constant Jacobian matrix.
